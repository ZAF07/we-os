## 0. Strategic Summary

Coast Coffee occupies a position **no existing Singapore competitor owns**: the intersection of **exclusively Asian-origin beans**, a **curated 3-tier taste-matching system**, and **engineered freshness (degassed + vacuum-sealed + guaranteed ≥6 weeks)**.

This brand strategy is designed to serve a single business objective: **acquire 70 new customers in 8 weeks**. It does so by creating a distinct category space where Coast Coffee does not compete on reputation (its weakness — Research §2.2) but instead competes on curation, taste-matching, and provable freshness — three dimensions where it has STRONG differentiation. Every section below explains how the recommendation removes a barrier that would otherwise prevent a Singapore home barista from making their first purchase.

## 1. Positioning

### 1.1 Positioning Statement

> **For** Singapore home baristas who want beans matched to their taste and delivered fresh,  
> **Coast Coffee is** the specialty coffee brand that **curates and guarantees** Asian-origin beans  
> **because** every bean is tested, placed in a 3-tier flavour system that matches your adventurousness, and vacuum-sealed after degassing so freshness lasts 6+ weeks — no guesswork, no stale bags, no trip to the roaster.

### 1.2 Positioning Map — Where Coast Coffee Fits

The strategy maps Coast Coffee against two axes that define the Singapore specialty DTC landscape:

```
                     CURATED / GUIDED
                          |
     Hook Coffee ●        |    ● Coast Coffee
     (subscription,        |    (Asian origins,
      global origins)      |     3-tier framework,
                           |     freshness guarantee)
                          |
    — ASIAN FOCUS ———+——— GLOBAL FOCUS —
                          |
     (Few/none at         |    ● Nylon, Tiong Hoe,
      this intersection)  |      Common Man, PPP,
                          |      Dutch Colony
                          |    (craft heritage,
                          |     global origins,
                          |     roast-date trust)
                          |
                   SELF-NAVIGATE / LIST-BASED
```

**Why this position (grounded in Research §2.2) — and how it enables 70 new customers:**

- The competitive audit found that Asian-origin focus is a **STRONG and ownable differentiation** — no major Singapore roaster or subscription service specialises exclusively in Asian beans (Research §2.1, §2.2). The established artisan roasters (Nylon, Tiong Hoe, Common Man, PPP, Dutch Colony) are global-origin generalists competing on craft heritage and barista reputation. Subscription players (Hook, Cowpresso, Morning) compete on convenience and discovery but also default to global origins. Coast Coffee is alone at the Asian-origin + curated intersection. **This matters for customer acquisition because it gives a curious home barista a reason to try Coast Coffee *in addition to*, not *instead of*, their existing roaster — lowering the switching cost that blocks trial.**

- The 3-tier curation model is also rated **STRONG differentiation** vs. the standard "list of origins + roast level" approach (Research §2.2). It directly addresses the **paradox-of-choice pain point** (Research §1.2), which the research rated MEDIUM-HIGH severity — home baristas default to known origins rather than risk a bad purchase. **This matters for the 70-customer goal because choice paralysis is a conversion killer: every visitor who leaves without buying because they "didn't know what to pick" is a lost acquisition. The tier system gives them a decision shortcut.**

- The freshness system (degassed + vacuum-sealed + ≥6-week guarantee) is rated **STRONG differentiation** vs. the market norm of "roast date only" (Research §2.2). This directly counters the **HIGH-severity freshness-uncertainty pain point** — the core trust barrier for mail-order beans (Research §1.2). **This matters for the 70-customer goal because freshness anxiety is the #1 reason a Singapore home barista who is curious about Coast Coffee will still not buy (Research §1.3: Evaluate stage). Removing this objection is a precondition for hitting the conversion rate needed to reach 70 customers from a limited budget.**

- Positioning at this intersection avoids competing where Coast Coffee is weakest: **brand maturity and reputation** (Research §2.2 rates this a WEAKNESS). The strategy does not try to out-heritage the established roasters or out-convenience the subscription apps. It creates a new category space: *the trusted guide to Asian specialty coffee for home baristas*. **This matters for the 70-customer goal because a new brand cannot win a reputation war in 8 weeks — it must change the terms of comparison so that reputation is not the deciding factor.**

### 1.3 Category Frame

Coast Coffee should frame itself not as "another specialty roaster" (an overcrowded mental category in Singapore) but as a **curated coffee discovery brand for Asian origins**. The mental model is closer to a trusted sommelier or a tasting club than a commodity roaster. This framing:

- Sets different expectations than a traditional roaster (you're not comparing Coast Coffee to Nylon's 10-year reputation; you're comparing it to "who helps me discover great Asian beans?")
- Elevates the 3-tier system from a "feature" to the core product architecture
- Justifies the premium price through curation labour, not just bean cost (Research §4.1: "curation-as-service" trend)

**Why — and how this enables 70 new customers:** The trend analysis (Research §4.1) identifies curation-as-service as a growing consumer expectation across categories (wine clubs, Stitch Fix). Coast Coffee's tier system maps directly onto this proven model. Meanwhile, the "rise of Asian-origin specialty coffee" trend gives the category frame tailwind. **For customer acquisition, this category frame is critical: it sets the expectation that Coast Coffee is *different* before the prospect even evaluates price or freshness. Without this frame, Coast Coffee is compared directly to established roasters and loses on reputation. With it, Coast Coffee is evaluated on a new set of criteria it can win.**

### 1.4 Positioning vs. Key Objections

| Customer Objection (Research §1.2) | Positioning Response | Acquisition Impact |
|-------------------------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| "Are Asian beans as good as the African/Latin origins I know?" | The tier system and "tested" claim signal quality control. Framing as *curated discovery* (not *budget alternative*) reframes Asian origins as interesting, not inferior. | This objection, if unaddressed, eliminates Segments B and C (~50% of target) — the very segments with highest LTV potential. |
| "Will the beans be fresh when they arrive?" | The vacuum-seal + ≥6-week guarantee is a specific, provable counter-claim — not "trust us," but "here's the system." | This is the #1 blocker across all four segments. Neutralising it is a prerequisite for any acquisition at all. |
| "Can I trust a new, small brand?" | Specific process claims (testing, degassing, vacuum-sealing) are more credible than vague quality promises. Transparency substitutes for reputation until reputation is earned. | The trust objection is HIGH severity for first purchase (Research §1.2). Until it is answered, conversion rate will not reach the ≥3% needed (Goal document). |
| "Why SGD 18–30 when supermarket is cheaper?" | Positioning as *curated discovery* rather than *commodity beans* justifies the premium. The trio offer at ~SGD 50–54 frames it as an affordable exploration vs. buying 3 full-price bags blind. | Price objection gates the widest segment (Segment A, ~35%). Without a premium justification, CAC rises because more paid impressions are needed per conversion. |

## 2. Messaging Architecture

### 2.1 Core Brand Message

> **Great Asian coffee, matched to your taste, guaranteed fresh.**

**Why this message (grounded in Research §1.1–§1.2 + §4.1) — and how it serves the 70-customer goal:**

This single sentence answers the four highest-severity customer anxieties — the anxieties that, left unaddressed, would prevent a visitor from becoming one of the 70 new customers:

1. "Asian coffee" → directly addresses the **unfamiliar-origins scepticism** (MEDIUM severity) by proudly claiming the category rather than hedging. Supported by the macro trend of rising Asian-origin specialty coffee (Research §4.1). **Acquisition logic: if the brand hedges on its own origin focus, prospects will too. A confident claim signals quality and reduces the "are these beans any good?" doubt at the Evaluate stage (Research §1.3).**

2. "Matched to your taste" → solves the **paradox of choice** (MEDIUM-HIGH severity) by signalling a system that does the work for the customer. This is the 3-tier framework in plain language. **Acquisition logic: choice paralysis stops purchases before they happen. "Matched to your taste" is a promise that short-circuits the indecision that costs conversions — and each lost conversion is a customer that must be replaced with additional paid spend (at a CAC target of ≤ SGD 14).**

3. "Guaranteed fresh" → directly counters the **freshness uncertainty** (HIGH severity), the top trust barrier for mail-order beans. **Acquisition logic: this is the objection that kills the most purchases at the final moment. Research §1.2 rates it HIGH — it is not a "nice to address" but a "must neutralise" for any acquisition to occur.**

4. The sentence's structure (origin → curation → assurance) mirrors the purchase decision chain identified in Research §1.3: Evaluate (origin? freshness?) → Decide (trust + value signal). **Acquisition logic: a message that follows the customer's own decision sequence reduces cognitive friction. The faster a prospect moves from Evaluate to Decide, the lower the effective CAC.**

### 2.2 Message Hierarchy

```
LEVEL 1 — BRAND PROMISE (emotional hook)
  "Discover Asia's best-kept coffee secrets, delivered to your brew bar."
  → Note: This exact phrasing is illustrative — it should be pressure-tested during
    creative development, not treated as the final locked message. The rationale
    (below) describes the strategic job this level must do regardless of final wording.
  → Why: Taps the "discovery" JTBD (Research §1.1, Segment B — Flavour Explorer),
    the "drink regional" trend (Research §4.1),
    and the Identity Brewer's need for uniqueness (Research §5, Segment C).
    Emotional, not functional. Opens curiosity.
  → Acquisition role: The emotional hook earns attention and interest — the top of
    the funnel that feeds the 70-customer pipeline. Without curiosity, there is no click.
    With a SGD 1,000 budget, every impression must earn the right to the next one.

LEVEL 2 — VALUE PROPOSITION (rational choice)
  "A 3-tier flavour system that matches beans to your taste —
   tested, vacuum-sealed, and guaranteed fresh for 6+ weeks."
  → Why: The three-pillar proof structure (tier system + testing + freshness guarantee)
    maps to the three highest-severity pain points (Research §1.2).
    This is the "reason to switch" for all four segments.
  → Acquisition role: This is the conversion message — the rational justification
    that turns interest into purchase. It answers "why should I buy *this*?" at the
    Decide stage (Research §1.3), where 70 purchase decisions must be won.

LEVEL 3 — SUPPORTING PROOF POINTS (credibility)
  → Why: Each maps to a specific doubt in the purchase decision chain (Research §1.3).
  → Acquisition role: Proof points close the gap between "I'm interested" and "I'm buying."
    For a new brand with no testimonials (DNA: `<TO CONFIRM>`), these specificity-driven
    proof points carry the credibility workload. Each one removes a reason to say no.
```

| Message | Targets | Answers This Objection | Research Ground |
|---------|---------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **"Beans from Asia's most interesting growing regions — Yunnan, Doi Chang, Gayo, Kintamani. Tested, not guessed."** | Segments B, C (Flavour Explorer, Identity Brewer) | "Are Asian origins as good as what I know?" | §4.1 Asian-origin trend; §1.2 unfamiliar-origins scepticism |
| **"Pick your tier. Brew with confidence. Three flavour levels, zero confusion."** | Segments A, D (Safe Brewer, Convenience Seeker) | "I don't know what to pick." | §1.2 paradox of choice; §5 — Tier 1 as low-barrier entry for Safe Brewers |
| **"Degassed. Vacuum-sealed. Fresh for 6+ weeks. That's not a promise — it's a process."** | All segments | "Will the beans actually be fresh?" | §1.2 HIGH-severity freshness anxiety; §2.2 freshness system as STRONG differentiation |
| **"Delivered to your door. No trip to the roaster, no queue, no carrying heavy bags."** | Segment D (Convenience Seeker) | "Why switch from my current delivery?" | §1.1 convenience JTBD; §2.2 delivery as table-stakes but a win vs. travel-to-buy |
| **"Try all three tiers in one go — our Flavour Trio gives you a taste of what Coast Coffee is about."** | Segments A, B (Safe Brewer, Flavour Explorer) | "I'm curious but don't want to commit to a full bag of something I might hate." | §5 — Trio offer prioritisation for Segments A+B; Goal document trio offer |

### 2.4 The Trio Offer — Messaging Role

The trio offer (~SGD 50–54 for one bag from each tier) is not just a promotion — it is the **messaging vehicle for the entire positioning**. It:

- **Demonstrates the tier system** rather than explaining it (show, don't tell)
- **Lowers risk** for Safe Brewers (Tier 1 is their anchor; Tiers 2 and 3 are "bonus discovery")
- **Satisfies variety craving** for Flavour Explorers (three distinct profiles)
- **Creates a natural upgrade path** — customers who buy the trio self-identify which tier they prefer, enabling targeted re-marketing

**Why — and how this serves the 70-customer goal:** Research §5 recommends prioritising Flavour Explorers (HIGHEST) and Safe Brewers (HIGH) for this campaign. The trio offer is the optimal conversion tool for both — it gives Safe Brewers permission to explore (converting the largest segment, ~35% of target) and gives Flavour Explorers the variety they want with a Tier 3 "bonus" that signals exclusivity (converting the highest-LTV segment, ~30% of target). Together, these two segments represent ~65% of the 70-customer target — roughly 45 customers. The trio also raises AOV to ~SGD 50–54 vs. a single-bag SGD 18–30, which means fewer total conversions are needed to hit the business KPI if trio share is high. At SGD 50+ AOV, even 40 trio conversions contribute SGD 2,000+ in revenue against a SGD 1,000 budget.

## 3. Brand Personality & Voice

> **Note:** The Customer DNA lists brand voice as `<TO CONFIRM>`. The entire voice and personality section below is a **recommendation — to be validated with the customer** before Creative Direction locks assets. No research directly measures Singapore home baristas' tonal preferences; the traits below are inferred from segment needs and pain points. Validation is essential: a misjudged tone can undermine the trust-building this strategy depends on.

### 3.1 Brand Personality

Coast Coffee should embody three personality traits. **All three are recommendations pending customer validation.**

| Trait | What It Means | Why (Research Ground) | Validation Status |
|-------|--------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Knowledgeable, not pretentious** | Confident about Asian origins and brewing, but never gatekeeping or intimidating. Like a barista who's excited to share what they know, not one who corrects your pronunciation. | Safe Brewer segment (Research §5, ~35% of target) needs *risk reduction*, not *more reasons to feel inadequate*. A pretentious or gatekeeping tone would increase perceived risk for this segment — the opposite of what the acquisition strategy requires. Meanwhile, the Flavour Explorer and Identity Brewer segments (combined ~50%) have enough coffee knowledge to detect condescension but respond to peer-level expertise. | **Recommended — validate with customer.** No direct survey or interview data confirms this tone preference. |
| **Curious and adventurous** | Energetic about discovery — new origins, unusual processing methods, unexpected flavour notes. The brand is a fellow explorer, not a lecturing professor. | This directly serves the Flavour Explorer and Identity Brewer segments (Research §5), whose dominant JTBD is discovery. It also aligns with the macro trend of "rise of Asian-origin specialty coffee" (Research §4.1) — Coast Coffee is telling a story that's still being written, not reciting established dogma. | **Recommended — validate with customer.** The discovery JTBD supports adventurousness, but the specific expression (fellow explorer vs. expert guide) has not been tested. |
| **Trustworthy through transparency** | Shows the process, names the numbers (6 weeks), explains the "why." Earns trust through specificity — never vague quality claims. | The #1 barrier for a new brand is trust (Research §1.2: HIGH severity, "trust in a new brand"). Coast Coffee cannot out-reputation the established roasters in 8 weeks, but it can out-transparency them. Process-driven claims (degassing, vacuum-sealing, testing) are more credible than heritage claims for a new entrant — and credibility directly enables the conversions needed for 70 new customers. | **Recommended — validate with customer.** The strategic logic is strong, but the customer should confirm they are comfortable with this level of process transparency and have the operational consistency to back it. |

**How these traits serve the 70-customer goal:** The trust barrier (Research §1.2: HIGH) is the single biggest obstacle between a curious visitor and a first purchase. These three traits are designed to lower that barrier through different mechanisms: approachability (reduces intimidation), excitement (creates desire), and transparency (substitutes for reputation). A brand that feels knowledgeable but warm, curious but credible, and transparent in its claims is a brand a Safe Brewer will try and a Flavour Explorer will buy from twice.

### 3.2 Brand Voice Guidelines

| Dimension | Do | Don't | Why |
|-----------|-----|-------|-----|
| **Tone** | Warm, confident, curious | Hype-y, overly salesy, or artificially urgent | The purchase cycle for specialty beans is considered, not impulse (Research §1.3). Hard-sell language undermines the "trusted guide" positioning and pushes away the Safe Brewer segment — the largest target (~35%) and the one most sensitive to pressure. |
| **Language level** | Accessible but precise — use correct coffee terms (degassing, processing method, varietal) but explain them briefly when they first appear | Jargon-dense without context; dumbed-down | The target is home baristas who know coffee but may not know Asian origins or processing nuances. Respect their knowledge; fill their gaps. Getting this wrong in either direction costs conversions. |
| **Specificity** | Name origins, processes, numbers ("vacuum-sealed after 48-hour degassing," "harvested October 2024 from Doi Chang at 1,400m") | Vague superlatives ("the best beans," "amazing quality," "premium") | Specificity = credibility for a new brand (Research §1.2: trust objection). Process storytelling is a proven content engine (Research §4.1: transparency trend). Every vague claim is a missed opportunity to build the trust that converts. |
| **Cultural register** | Singapore-colloquial-friendly but not forced. Natural Singlish is fine if the founder uses it authentically. | Fake-relatable corporate speak; trying too hard to sound "local" | Authenticity matters. If the founder is Singaporean, let it show naturally. If not, don't force Singlish — the audience will smell it. English is the confirmed primary language (DNA). Inauthenticity = trust erosion = higher CAC. |
| **Relationship to coffee culture** | Enthusiastic participant — "we're on this journey too" | Gatekeeper or final authority — "this is how it's done" | The Identity Brewer segment (Research §5) follows roasters who position as peers, not authorities. The brand should feel like a knowledgeable friend, not an institution. An authoritative tone would lose the highest-LTV segment before they even consider buying. |

### 3.3 Voice in Practice — Before/After Examples

| Context | Don't (generic/vague) | Do (Coast Coffee voice) |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Describing freshness | "Our beans are always fresh." | "We rest every batch 48 hours after roast, then vacuum-seal. Open a bag 4 weeks later — it still pops with aroma. That's the process, not luck." |
| Introducing Asian origins | "We source the finest Asian coffee beans." | "Yunnan gets called 'the next Colombia' by Q-graders who've cupped it blind. We tested that claim ourselves before putting it in Tier 2." |
| Explaining the tiers | "We have three flavour levels to suit every palate." | "Tier 1 is your daily driver — chocolatey, balanced, impossible to brew wrong. Tier 3 is for Saturday mornings when you want your V60 to surprise you." |
| Trio offer | "Buy our bundle and save!" | "One bag from each tier. Brew them side by side. By the third cup, you'll know exactly which Coast Coffee is yours." |

## 4. Value Proposition

### 4.1 Value Proposition Statement

> **Coast Coffee makes discovering Asian specialty coffee risk-free.**  
> A 3-tier flavour system matches beans to your taste — not your ability to decode tasting notes.  
> Every bag is tested, degassed, and vacuum-sealed — guaranteed fresh for 6+ weeks.  
> Delivered to your door in Singapore. No guesswork. No stale beans. No trip to the roaster.

**How this serves the 70-customer goal:** This value proposition is structured as a conversion engine. Each element neutralises a specific objection in the purchase decision chain (Research §1.3, Evaluate → Decide). The goal of 70 new customers in 8 weeks with a SGD 1,000 budget requires a conversion rate ≥3% (Goal document) — meaning for every 100 visitors, at least 3 must buy. This value prop is designed to maximise that rate by answering the objections that would otherwise cause a visitor to leave without purchasing.

### 4.2 Value Proposition Deconstruction

| Element | What It Is | Why It Matters (Research Ground) | Acquisition Impact |
|---------|-----------|----------------------------------|---------------------|
| **"Discovering Asian specialty coffee"** | The core promise — discovery, not just supply | Taps the discovery JTBD (Research §1.1) and the Asian-origin trend (Research §4.1). Frames the brand as a category creator, not a commodity seller. | Creates the *reason to visit* — the top-of-funnel pull that feeds the 70-customer pipeline. Without a distinct promise, paid spend fights for attention in a crowded specialty market. |
| **"Risk-free"** | The emotional guarantee | Directly addresses the HIGH-severity trust barrier (Research §1.2) and the Safe Brewer's dominant need for risk reduction (Research §5, Segment A). | This is the conversion unlock for the largest segment (~35%). Safe Brewers are the most price-sensitive to CAC because they're the most hesitant — removing their fear is the difference between a 2% and 3%+ conversion rate. |
| **"3-tier flavour system matches beans to your taste"** | The mechanism — how risk is removed | Answers the paradox-of-choice pain point (Research §1.2, MEDIUM-HIGH). Differentiates from every competitor whose model is "here's a list, good luck" (Research §2.2). | Reduces bounce rate at the Evaluate stage. Every visitor who leaves because they "didn't know what to pick" is a wasted impression — and at SGD 125/week, wasted impressions directly reduce the number of customers acquired. |
| **"Tested, degassed, and vacuum-sealed — guaranteed fresh for 6+ weeks"** | The tangible proof — why the guarantee is credible | Counters the #1 freshness anxiety (Research §1.2, HIGH). The specificity of the process claim is what makes it trustworthy for a new brand with no reputation yet (Research §2.2: specificity substitutes for heritage). | Neutralises the #1 purchase blocker. Freshness anxiety is rated HIGH severity — it is not a secondary concern but the primary reason a prospect says no. Removing it is a prerequisite for every one of the 70 conversions. |
| **"Delivered to your door in Singapore"** | The convenience wrapper | Serves the convenience JTBD (Research §1.1). Table-stakes vs. other DTC brands (Research §2.2) but a genuine advantage vs. the many established roasters with limited/no delivery. | Converts the Convenience Seeker segment (~15%) and removes a friction point for all others. Each removed friction point marginally improves conversion rate. |
| **"No guesswork. No stale beans. No trip to the roaster."** | The three objections neutralised in one breath | Each phrase maps to a specific pain point: paradox of choice, freshness uncertainty, convenience (Research §1.2). The triad structure is memorable and complete. | This is the close — the final push past hesitation. It is structured so that whichever objection was still lingering for a given prospect, it's answered in this line. |
