## 1. Positioning

### 1.1 Positioning Statement

> **For** Singapore home baristas who want specialty beans delivered — but worry about freshness and don't know which origin to pick —  
> **Coast Coffee is** the specialty coffee brand  
> **that brings you** Asia's most distinctive beans, curated by adventurousness into three simple tiers, and vacuum-sealed for freshness you can count on.  
> **Because** we test every bean before it earns a tier, degas and vacuum-seal every bag after roasting, and guarantee six weeks of freshness — so you explore with confidence, not guesswork.

### 1.2 Why This Positioning

This positioning is built from three research findings that, taken together, describe a space no competitor currently owns:

| Positioning element | Grounded in | Research source |
|---------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Asian-origin exclusivity** | No major Singapore competitor specialises exclusively in Asian beans. The "rise of Asian-origin specialty coffee" is a GROWING trend. | Research §2.2 (competitive positioning map), §4.1 (trend analysis) |
| **Curation by adventurousness** | No competitor uses a tiered-flavour-adventurousness framework. The "paradox of choice" pain point is MEDIUM-HIGH severity. The curation-as-service trend is GROWING. | Research §2.2, §1.2, §4.1; Observations #5 |
| **Provable freshness** | Freshness anxiety is the HIGHEST severity pain point across all four segments. The degassing + vacuum-sealing + ≥6-week guarantee is a STRONG differentiator vs. the market norm of "roast date only." | Research §1.2, §2.2; Observations #2 |

The three elements reinforce each other: Asian origins create curiosity (the *what*), curation removes risk (the *how to choose*), and the freshness guarantee removes the final purchase blocker (the *can I trust this by mail?*). Positioning that omits any one leg leaves a vulnerability: Asian origins without curation = overwhelming; curation without freshness = untrustworthy; freshness without Asian origins = undifferentiated.

Positioning here creates a defensible, ownable space.

## 2. Messaging

### 2.1 Core Message

> **Asia's best beans. Curated for your taste. Sealed for freshness you can count on.**

This single sentence carries all three positioning pillars. It answers the three questions every prospective buyer asks (per the purchase decision chain, Research §1.3): *What am I getting?* (Asia's best beans), *How do I choose?* (curated for my taste), and *Can I trust it?* (sealed for freshness I can count on).

### 2.2 Why This Core Message

It is designed to neutralise the two highest-severity pain points identified in research:

- **Freshness anxiety** (HIGH, §1.2): "Sealed for freshness you can count on" directly addresses the #1 purchase blocker. The word "sealed" is specific — it implies a process (vacuum-sealing), not a vague promise.
- **Paradox of choice** (MEDIUM-HIGH, §1.2): "Curated for your taste" signals that Coast Coffee does the filtering work. The buyer doesn't need to decode dozens of origins — the tier system has already done it.

And it leans into the strongest differentiation: "Asia's best beans" is a claim no Singapore competitor can make because none specialise exclusively in Asian origins (§2.2).

### 2.3 Messaging Pillars

Each pillar supports the core message and answers a specific customer objection (from DNA §Common objections and Research §1.2).

#### Pillar 1 — "You pick the tier. We've done the rest."

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **What it says** | Three tiers. No guesswork. Each bean tested before it earns its place. |
| **Objection it answers** | "I don't know what to pick" / "What if I buy a bag I hate?" |
| **Research grounding** | Paradox of choice — MEDIUM-HIGH severity (§1.2). No competitor offers a tiered-flavour-adventurousness model (§2.2). The trio offer maps naturally to Segments A (Safe Brewer) and B (Flavour Explorer) — Observations #1. |
| **Segments it speaks to** | Primarily A and B; also C (who will gravitate to Tier 3). |

#### Pillar 2 — "Vacuum-sealed. Degassed. Fresh for six weeks. Guaranteed."

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **What it says** | A named, specific process — not "we care about freshness," but "here's what we do and here's the number." |
| **Objection it answers** | "Will the beans actually be fresh when they arrive?" / "Can I trust a new, small brand?" |
| **Research grounding** | Freshness anxiety — HIGH severity across ALL segments (§1.2). Trust is the binding constraint for a new brand — Observations #4. The purchase decision chain requires both a trust signal AND a value signal (§1.3). This pillar delivers the trust signal. |
| **Segments it speaks to** | All four. Freshness is the universal purchase blocker. |

#### Pillar 3 — "Origins you won't find everywhere. Stories worth sharing."

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **What it says** | China, Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia — beans with provenance and personality. Not commodity. Not generic. |
| **Objection it answers** | "Are Asian origins as good as the African/Latin beans I know?" / "Is this genuinely special or just marketed that way?" |
| **Research grounding** | Unfamiliar Asian origins — MEDIUM scepticism (§1.2). The rise of Asian-origin specialty coffee is a GROWING trend (§4.1). "Drink regional" sentiment is EMERGING (§4.1). The "Asian beans are lower quality" stigma is a real counter-trend (§4.2) — this pillar must do active re-education work, not just make a claim. |
| **Segments it speaks to** | Primarily B and C; A will listen once trust is established through Tier 1. |

#### Pillar 4 — "From our roaster to your door."

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **What it says** | Delivery is core to the model. You don't need to travel. |
| **Objection it answers** | "Why switch from my current roaster/subscription?" (Segment D) |
| **Research grounding** | Delivery is TABLE-STAKES in Singapore (§2.2) — this pillar is necessary but not differentiating. It exists so the convenience promise isn't absent, but it should never lead the message. Convenience is why someone stays; it is not why someone switches. |
| **Segments it speaks to** | Primarily D; a hygiene factor for A, B, and C. |

## 3. Brand Personality & Voice

> **Status:** PROVISIONAL — derived inferentially from the Customer DNA and Market Research. The DNA marks brand voice as `<TO CONFIRM>`. These voice attributes, do's/don'ts, and tone guidelines are the agent's best reading of what the brand should sound like given its positioning, audience, and differentiation. **The customer should validate, revise, or replace this section before creative work begins.**

### 3.1 Why This Voice

The voice is not chosen for aesthetic preference — it is derived from three strategic inputs:

| Input | What it demands of the voice |
|-------|------------------------------|
| **The purchase decision chain requires a trust signal before anything else** (Research §1.3, Observations #4) | The voice must sound credible, specific, and transparent — never vague or hype-y. Trust is earned in the details. |
| **The brand is a new entrant competing against multi-year institutions** (Research §2.2 — brand maturity = WEAKNESS) | The voice cannot posture as an established authority ("we've been roasting since…"). It must earn authority through demonstrated expertise, process transparency, and warmth — the tone of a knowledgeable guide, not a gatekeeper. |
| **The audience spans Safe Brewer through Identity Brewer** (Research §5, Segments A–D) | The voice must flex across segments: reassuring and simple for A, excited and knowledgeable for B, credible and insider-aware for C, frictionless and direct for D. It cannot be a single fixed register. |

### 3.2 Brand Archetype

**Primary archetype: The Curator (Explorer-Sage hybrid)**

Coast Coffee doesn't just sell beans — it selects, tests, and organises them so the customer doesn't have to. This is the Curator's job: "I've done the work so you can enjoy the result with confidence."

Why not the Sage alone: The Sage posture ("I know more than you") risks alienating the Safe Brewer and Convenience Seeker. The Curator is more generous — "I found these; I tested them; here's what I think you'll like."

Why not the Explorer alone: The Explorer posture ("come adventuring with me") doesn't address the trust gap. New brands can't lead an expedition until they've proven they can read a map. The Curator earns the right to be adventurous.

### 3.3 Voice Attributes

| Attribute | What it means | Why this attribute |
|-----------|---------------|--------------------|
| **Warm, not clinical** | Write like a person who loves coffee talking to another person who loves coffee. Use natural sentence rhythms. No corporate-speak. | The brand has no institutional gravitas to lean on — warmth is how a new brand builds affinity. The Curator archetype is relational, not transactional. |
| **Specific, not vague** | Name the process (degassed, vacuum-sealed), the number (six weeks), the origin (Yunnan, not "Asia"). Specificity is credibility. | Freshness anxiety (HIGH, §1.2) and new-brand trust deficit (Observations #4) are neutralised by concrete detail, not by adjectives. "We care about freshness" is meaningless; "we vacuum-seal after degassing — you get six weeks guaranteed" is an argument. |
| **Confident, not boastful** | Assert what's true (tested, sealed, curated) without overclaiming. Let the process speak. No superlatives without proof. | The Identity Brewer (Segment C) has a high bar for credibility. The Safe Brewer (Segment A) is sceptical of marketing language. Confidence backed by process earns both. |
| **Curious, not preachy** | Share origin stories with genuine fascination. Invite the reader into discovery. Never lecture about "proper" brewing. | The Flavour Explorer (Segment B) wants discovery, not instruction. The brand's Asian-origin thesis is inherently interesting — let the beans be interesting rather than insisting they are. |
| **Accessible, not dumbed-down** | Use plain language but assume intelligence. Explain terms when needed ("degassing means…") without patronising. | The audience spans new home baristas and prosumer espresso owners. The voice must work for both — clear enough for Segment A, credible enough for Segment C. |

### 3.4 Voice Do's and Don'ts

#### DO:

- **DO** use concrete, sensory tasting notes: "stonefruit sweetness, like a ripe yellow peach" over "fruity notes."
- **DO** name the process steps: "We rest the beans 48 hours after roasting so CO₂ escapes — that's degassing — then vacuum-seal them."
- **DO** frame Asian origins as discoveries: "Yunnan is making waves in specialty circles — here's why this lot caught our attention."
- **DO** acknowledge the reader's knowledge without assuming it: "If you've brewed single-origins before, you'll recognise this as a washed process — clean, bright, structured. If that's new to you: it means the fruit was removed from the bean before drying, which gives you those crisp, clear flavours."
- **DO** use the tier names (Tier 1 / Tier 2 / Tier 3 or their eventual branded equivalents) consistently — they are the navigation system.
- **DO** lead with what the customer gets, not what the brand does: "You get beans sealed for six weeks of freshness" over "We vacuum-seal our beans."
- **DO** use Singapore-appropriate register: "kopi" and local brewing references are fine if natural; don't force Singlish for credibility.
- **DO** let numbers do the work: "Six weeks guaranteed," "Three tiers, no guesswork," "SGD 1.50–2.50 a cup."

#### DON'T:

- **DON'T** use empty freshness language: "farm-to-cup," "artisanal," "handcrafted," "small-batch" — these are table-stakes in specialty coffee and signal nothing. Coast Coffee's claim is stronger and more specific.
- **DON'T** make claims you can't prove. "The best beans in Asia" is unprovable. "Beans we've tested and stand behind" is provable.
- **DON'T** disparage African or Latin American origins to elevate Asian ones. The audience likely loves those origins. Position Asian beans as *another* source of great coffee, not a *replacement*.
- **DON'T** use snobbery or gatekeeping language: no "if you were a *real* coffee person," no sneering at supermarket beans or Nespresso. The Safe Brewer may use both. Meet them where they are.
- **DON'T** over-explain. If a sentence can be seven words instead of seventeen, use seven. The Convenience Seeker skims.
- **DON'T** use jargon without context. "Washed Bourbon from Doi Chang" means nothing to a Safe Brewer. "A clean, bright coffee from northern Thailand" does.
- **DON'T** lead with the brand story ("we started because…"). The audience doesn't know or trust the brand yet. Lead with what they get — the brand story is supporting evidence, not the opener.
- **DON'T** use urgency or scarcity tactics ("only 50 bags left!"). They erode trust for a new brand. If a bean is genuinely limited, state the fact: "This lot was 12 sacks. When it's gone, it's gone." No exclamation marks on scarcity.

### 3.5 Tone Calibration by Segment

The voice attributes remain consistent across segments, but emphasis and register shift:

| Segment | Tone shift | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **A — Safe Brewer** | Lead with reassurance. Warmth and simplicity dominate. Process details are presented as safety, not science. | "Tier 1 is our 'tastes like what you love' tier. Familiar flavours, clean, balanced. If you like a classic Colombian or Brazilian cup, start here." |
| **B — Flavour Explorer** | Lead with discovery. Specificity and curiosity dominate. Process details are presented as interesting, not just reassuring. | "This Yunnan lot is all jasmine and mandarin — floral, bright, totally unexpected. We cupped it blind against a washed Ethiopian and it held its own. Tier 2 material." |
| **C — Identity Brewer** | Lead with credibility. Specificity and confidence dominate. Skip the explanations they don't need. Give them data: variety, process, elevation, producer. | "Anaerobic natural from Doi Chang, Thailand. 1,400 MASL. Caturra and Typica. Fermented 72 hours in sealed tanks. Tastes like rum-soaked cherries and dark chocolate. Tier 3." |
| **D — Convenience Seeker** | Lead with speed. Accessibility dominates. The fewest words to convey: this is good, fresh, easy, delivered. | "Pick a tier. We'll deliver fresh, vacuum-sealed beans. Six weeks guaranteed. Done." |

## 4. Value Proposition

### 4.1 Value Proposition Statement

> **Coast Coffee gives Singapore home baristas a better way to buy beans:**  
> A curated 3-tier system so you pick with confidence, not guesswork.  
> Vacuum-sealed freshness you can count on for six weeks — guaranteed.  
> And access to Asia's most distinctive origins, delivered to your door.  
> All at ~SGD 1.50–2.50 per cup — café quality without the café price.

### 4.2 Why This Value Proposition

Each element maps to a validated customer need from the research:

| Value element | Customer need it serves | Research grounding |
|---------------|------------------------|--------------------|
| **Curated 3-tier system** | Risk reduction; freedom from choice paralysis | Paradox of choice — MEDIUM-HIGH severity (§1.2). Curation-as-service trend — GROWING (§4.1). |
| **Vacuum-sealed, ≥6-week freshness** | Trust in mail-order beans; no wasted stale beans | Freshness anxiety — HIGH severity across all segments (§1.2). The #1 purchase blocker (Observations #2). |
| **Distinctive Asian origins** | Discovery without guesswork; unique beans with stories | Asian-origin exclusivity is a white-space position (§2.2). "Drink regional" sentiment — EMERGING (§4.1). |
| **Home delivery** | Convenience; one less errand | Delivery is table-stakes (§2.2) — necessary but not differentiating. Included for completeness. |
| **~SGD 1.50–2.50/cup** | Affordable indulgence vs. café spend | Economic analysis (§3.2): compares favourably to SGD 5–8 café cups. Specialty coffee is an "affordable luxury" — resilient in downturns. |

### 4.3 Value Proposition by Segment

| Segment | Primary value hook | Why this hook for this segment |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| **A — Safe Brewer** | "Tier 1: tastes like what you love, from an origin you haven't tried. Six weeks fresh. Guaranteed." | Risk reduction is the dominant need. Freshness guarantee removes the mail-order fear. Tier 1 anchors the safe choice. |
| **B — Flavour Explorer** | "Three tiers, three flavour worlds. Asian origins curated by someone who's done the cupping." | Discovery with guardrails is the dominant need. The tier framework promises variety without danger. |
| **C — Identity Brewer** | "Beans most Singapore roasters don't stock. Tested, vacuum-sealed, provenance you can trace." | Uniqueness and credibility are the dominant needs. Asian exclusivity + process transparency earn their respect. |
| **D — Convenience Seeker** | "Pick a tier. Delivered. Fresh for six weeks. That's it." | Friction reduction is the dominant need. The simplest possible value statement. |

### 4.4 Value Proposition vs. Key Objections

The value proposition is designed to answer every inferred objection from the DNA:

| Objection (DNA) | Value proposition answer |
|-----------------|--------------------------|
| "Will the beans actually be fresh when they arrive?" | Vacuum-sealed after degassing. ≥6 weeks guaranteed. Specific process, not a vague promise. |
| "I don't know these Asian origins — are they as good?" | Tested before they earn a tier. Our reputation depends on every bag. Tier 1 is specifically designed to taste familiar. |
| "Why pay SGD 18–30 when the supermarket is cheaper?" | ~SGD 1.50–2.50/cup vs. SGD 5–8 at a café. You're upgrading your daily coffee, not splurging. Plus: freshness and curation the supermarket can't offer. |
| "Can I trust a new, small brand?" | The freshness guarantee has a number (six weeks). The vacuum seal is visible. The tier system shows rigour. Trust through transparency — we earn it with every bag. |
