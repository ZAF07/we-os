#### 1.3 Purchase Decision Chain (inferred)

```
Trigger (running low / want to try something new)
  → Research (social media, Google, word of mouth, specialty forums)
  → Evaluate (origin? freshness? price? reviews? curation?)
  → Decide (trust signal + value signal must both be met)
  → Buy (low-friction checkout, delivery expectation set)
  → Receive & Brew (freshness promise tested immediately)
  → Repeat or churn (first-bag experience determines lifetime value)
```

**Source:** Inferred from general DTC specialty-food purchase behaviour patterns. No Coast Coffee-specific funnel data exists.

#### 2.3 Competitive Forces (Porter's Five Forces — abridged)

| Force | Assessment | Evidence |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Direct rivalry** | HIGH | Singapore has a dense, high-quality specialty coffee scene. Many roasters are multi-year institutions with loyal followings. Low switching costs for consumers. |
| **Threat of new entrants** | MODERATE | Low barriers to start (sourcing, roasting, Shopify), but building trust/reputation is the real moat — which takes years, not weeks. |
| **Substitute threat** | HIGH | Supermarket beans, Nespresso, kopi from the coffeeshop, or simply sticking with a known roaster. The "do nothing" option (keep current behaviour) is the strongest competitor. |
| **Buyer power** | HIGH | Singapore consumers are well-informed, compare easily online via social media and chat groups, and have many alternatives. |
| **Supplier power** | MODERATE | Asian-origin specialty beans are less commoditised than African/Latin, which could mean more distinctive supply (advantage) or fewer supplier options (risk). |

#### 4.2 Counter-Trends / Risks

| Risk | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **"Asian beans are lower quality" stigma** | Older or more traditional specialty consumers may associate Asian origins with commodity-grade robusta (historically accurate for some regions). Coast Coffee's model (per-bag purchase with optional trio offer) partially sidesteps this vs. mandatory subscriptions, but the risk of being mentally filed as "another subscription box" exists. |

However, at-home specialty coffee typically weathers downturns better than café spending (consumers trade down from café visits to better home brewing). |

## 5. Audience Segmentation

### Framework: Needs-Based Segmentation (Haley, 1968) + Behavioural Overlay

The DNA identifies one primary segment: **Singapore home baristas who want curated, guaranteed-fresh Asian-origin beans delivered**. Within this, four sub-segments emerge based on brewing behaviour and psychological need:

#### Segment A: "The Safe Brewer" (~35% of target)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Profile** | Brews daily but sticks to what they know. Owns a V60 or French press. Buys the same 1–2 origins repeatedly. Curious about Asian beans but worried about wasting money on a bad bag. |
| **Dominant need** | Risk reduction — "don't let me buy a bag I'll hate." |
| **JTBD priority** | Get reliably good coffee every morning without surprises. |
| **Likely entry point** | Tier 1 (safe/basic) — "tastes like what you already love, but from an origin you haven't tried." |
| **Key objection** | "Why try something new when I know what works?" |
| **Estimated size** | Largest segment (~35% of target) — inferred; the "safe majority" pattern is common in specialty food adoption curves. |

#### Segment B: "The Flavour Explorer" (~30% of target)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Profile** | Brews 3–5×/week with multiple methods (pour-over, Aeropress, maybe entry-level espresso). Actively seeks new origins. Reads tasting notes. Follows roasters on Instagram. Already buys specialty beans online. |
| **Dominant need** | Discovery with guardrails — "surprise me, but don't disappoint me." |
| **JTBD priority** | Expand my palate with interesting beans someone knowledgeable has vetted. |
| **Likely entry point** | Tier 2 (complex/floral) — "the sweet spot between familiar and adventurous." |
| **Key objection** | "Are Asian origins genuinely interesting, or just a gimmick?" |
| **Estimated size** | ~30% of target — inferred; the "enthusiast explorer" segment is well-documented in specialty food categories. |

#### Segment C: "The Identity Brewer" (~20% of target)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Profile** | Coffee is part of their identity. Owns prosumer espresso gear or a full pour-over ritual. Shares brew photos on social media. Wants beans with a story — origin, producer, processing method. Follows international roasters. |
| **Dominant need** | Status and uniqueness — "I want beans nobody else has." |
| **JTBD priority** | Serve and share coffee that reflects my taste and knowledge. |
| **Likely entry point** | Tier 3 (exciting/unknown) — "the beans even most specialty shops don't stock." |
| **Key objection** | "Is this genuinely rare/special, or just marketed that way?" |
| **Estimated size** | ~20% of target — inferred; the "identity enthusiast" is a recognised specialty-coffee consumer segment but is definitionally a minority. |

#### Segment D: "The Convenience Seeker" (~15% of target)

| Dimension | Detail |

| **Profile** | Enjoys good coffee but won't travel to get it. Time-poor professional. Buys online. Wants the quality of a roaster with the ease of delivery. May already use a subscription service. Brewing method is whatever is fastest. |

| **JTBD priority** | Restock quality beans without leaving home or overthinking. |

## 6. Research Observations (Not Recommendations)

The following observations emerge from the findings above. They are signposts for downstream agents, not strategic directives:

1. **The trio offer maps naturally to Segments A and B.** The three-tier structure gives Safe Brewers a Tier 1 anchor (reducing perceived risk of the other two bags) and gives Flavour Explorers the variety they seek. This is a research observation about segment-product fit, not a campaign recommendation.

2. **Freshness anxiety is the single highest-severity pain point across all four segments.** Research §1.2 rates it HIGH. Any positioning or messaging that does not neutralise this objection leaves the primary purchase blocker in place — which the purchase decision chain (§1.3) suggests would suppress conversion at the Evaluate → Decide transition.

3. **Asian-origin exclusivity is a genuine white-space position.** Research §2.2 finds no major Singapore competitor specialising exclusively in Asian beans. Combined with the GROWING "rise of Asian-origin specialty coffee" trend (§4.1), this creates a positioning opportunity that is both distinctive and trend-supported.

4. **Trust is the binding constraint for a new brand.** Research §2.2 rates brand maturity as a WEAKNESS. The purchase decision chain (§1.3) shows that the Decide step requires both a trust signal and a value signal. Without an established reputation, Coast Coffee must earn trust through transparency, specificity, and provable process claims (the degassing + vacuum-sealing system).

5. **The curation model addresses a real and under-served pain point.** The paradox of choice is rated MEDIUM-HIGH severity (§1.2), and no competitor uses a tiered-flavour-adventurousness framework (§2.2). The curation-as-service trend (§4.1) provides macro-level validation that consumers value guided selection.
