## Executive summary

- **Positioning (carried forward, sharpened):** Coast Coffee is the **Asian-origin discovery roaster for Singapore home baristas** — the only one that organises beans into a **3-tier "safe → unknown" framework** and **guarantees freshness for ≥6 weeks** after degassing and vacuum-sealing, so brewing at home by mail stops being a gamble. **[PS, adapted]**
- **What's different for this campaign (70 vs. 50 target):** paid alone (~SGD 1,000) can drive only ~35–45 of the 70. The remaining **25–35 customers must come from organic/earned/community/referral channels**. **[RES]** This shifts the brand strategy's centre of gravity: positioning must be **pitchable to editors and shareable by early buyers**, not just optimised for paid-ad conversion. Trust — always the binding constraint — is now even more load-bearing because sceptics acquired organically lack the warming effect of a paid ad.
- **The trio sampler is the trust engine, not just the hero offer.** For a cold audience meeting Coast for the first time (especially via editorial or community referral), the trio's structure — three tiers, three distinct experiences, one low-risk purchase — *is* the risk-reversal. **[RES]** The strategy must make the trio feel like the obvious, safe, smart choice, not a gamble on three bags.
- **Three behavioural segments, three reasons to buy the trio.** The framework is unchanged but the messaging flex per segment is now more deliberate: explorers want novelty, upgraders want the tasting flight, and replenishers want a low-risk way to find their go-to bag. **[RES]** The replenisher — harder to reach via paid discovery — is now a must-convert, not a nice-to-have. Messaging must explicitly invite the cautious ("Start safe — Tier 1 is an easy, reliable cup") alongside the bold.
- **Trust is the binding constraint and there are still no testimonials.** The strategy compensates with risk-reversal (the guarantee as a promise the brand *keeps*) and radical process transparency, but also explicitly mandates a **mid-campaign social-proof harvest plan** — early reviews and UGC must be captured and deployed by week 4, because organic acquisition depends on credibility signals compounding. **[RES, ADAPT]**
- **Voice: the knowledgeable guide (provisional recommendation).** Expert but never gatekeeping; warm and plain-spoken; confident about the guarantee; curious and discovery-led; honest and specific. **DNA voice is `<TO CONFIRM>` — this section is an unresolved gap that requires customer validation before Creative locks in tone.** See Section 4. **[PS, REC — not yet binding]**

## 1. Positioning statement

*Framework: classic positioning statement. Core idea from prior strategy, adapted for the higher-target / organic-dependency context.*

> **For** Singapore home baristas who want to brew distinctive coffee at home and keep discovering new beans, **but** are wary of gambling on unfamiliar origins or stale mail-order beans,
> **Coast Coffee** is the **Asian-origin specialty coffee service**
> **that** lets them explore beans from across Asia — matched to exactly how adventurous they feel — with freshness they can count on,
> **because** every bag is sorted into a clear 3-tier framework (safe → complex → exciting), tested before it ships, and **guaranteed fresh for at least 6 weeks** after degassing and vacuum-sealing.

**Category we choose to compete in:** *Asian-origin discovery roaster / curated specialty-bean service* — deliberately **not** "another SG specialty roaster" (crowded field led by PPP and Common Man on global origins) and **not** a single-country heritage play (Tanamera, JJ Royal own Indonesia-as-heritage). **[RES, PS]**

**Why this positioning (six grounds, tied to the goal):**

1. **Whitespace is unchanged and defensible.** Research confirms no SG competitor unites multi-country Asian origin + 3-tier curation + a ≥6-week freshness guarantee. **[RES]** Coast must own all three together; any one alone is copyable. For a cold launch at a higher target, a *distinct* claim earns the click where a "me-too" claim loses to bigger brands.

2. **The "but they're wary…" clause is load-bearing for the 70-customer goal.** Research validates two persistent first-purchase frictions — freshness-on-arrival and unfamiliar origins. **[RES]** The positioning answers both objections *inside the claim itself*, lowering perceived risk for a first order. This matters more at 70 customers than at 50 because the campaign must convert more sceptics (including replenishers who are less novelty-driven).

3. **Unfamiliar origins → ahead of the curve (the reframe).** The Asian-coffee trend tailwind (Thailand/Yunnan rising, APAC fastest-growing) **[RES]** lets Coast turn its biggest apparent weakness into the reason to buy — a positioning that is inherently pitchable to editors ("The Asian-origin roaster riding the next wave of specialty coffee") and shareable by early adopters. This directly supports the organic/earned customer contribution the campaign depends on. **[RES, ADAPT]**

4. **Editorial and community channels are not downstream afterthoughts — the positioning must travel.** To acquire 25–35 customers organically, Coast's story must be easy for listicle writers, community roasters, and home-barista peers to retell. The "Asian-origin discovery" angle is a genuine trend story, not a manufactured one — it earns inclusion in "best roasters" roundups and community conversation without requiring paid placement. **[RES, ADAPT]**

5. **The 3-tier framework makes the positioning self-demonstrating.** A cold buyer doesn't need to trust Coast's taste judgment — the framework lets them *see* how beans are organised and *choose their own comfort level*. The positioning is provable the moment someone looks at the offering, which is critical when no testimonials exist.

6. **Deliberate non-claims:** cheapest (Hook owns it, from SGD 14), biggest/most-established (PPP owns "#1"), café heritage (Common Man owns it). Competing on those loses; Coast wins on a different axis entirely. **[RES, PS]**

## 3. Messaging hierarchy

*Framework: message pyramid — primary message → supporting messages → proof, with objection-handlers and segment-specific tier mapping.*

### Primary message (the one thing to remember)
**"Discover Asian coffee matched to your taste — guaranteed fresh, or it's on us."** **[PS]**
*Why:* fuses the differentiating idea (Asian discovery, curated to you) with the risk-reversal that compensates for the trust gap, in service of the first-purchase decision. The "or it's on us" framing is the unflinching confidence a small brand needs to earn a first order from a sceptic. **[RES]**

### Supporting messages
1. **"Start safe or go bold — you choose."** Communicates the 3-tier framework as an invitation, not a taxonomy. Serves the curation pillar. **[DNA #1, PS]**
2. **"Beans from China, Thailand, Indonesia and beyond — the origins most roasters skip."** Communicates the Asian-origin novelty and the trend-tailwind credibility. **[DNA #3; RES, PS]**
3. **"We test every bean, then guarantee it's fresh for at least 6 weeks."** Communicates the guarantee + testing as a concrete, checkable promise. **[DNA #2, PS]**
4. **"Try all three tiers in one box — find your favourite."** Communicates the trio as the low-risk way to experience the whole system. **[Goal offer, ADAPT]** — the addition of "find your favourite" broadens the frame from discovery-only to include the replenisher's job: *identify my go-to bag.*

### How messaging maps to the 3 flavour tiers (unchanged framework, adapted emphasis)
The tiers are a **messaging device**, not just a SKU list — each speaks to a different mindset and behavioural sub-segment. **[RES segments]**

| Tier | Message angle | Primary segment | Why this campaign **[ADAPT]** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — safe & basic** | *"A reliable, crowd-pleasing cup to start with — your daily go-to, delivered."* | **Replenishers** + nervous first-timers | Now explicitly positioned as a *routine anchor*, not just an on-ramp. The replenisher — needed to hit 70 — wants to find their daily bag, not just dip a toe. |
| **Tier 2 — complex & floral** | *"Something more nuanced when you're ready to explore."* | **Upgraders** (gear-led enthusiasts) | Rewards the engaged enthusiast who values nuance and brew control. Unchanged because the upgrader job is stable. |
| **Tier 3 — exciting & unknown** | *"The bean you can't get anywhere else — for the adventurous."* | **Flavour-adventurers** | The novelty/status payoff; the clearest expression of the Asian-discovery promise. The most shareable and pitchable tier for editorial/community. |

### How messaging maps to the trio sampler offer

The trio at ~SGD 50–54 is the **hero offer and the trust engine** — its job is to let a cold buyer experience the entire 3-tier system in one low-risk, single-decision purchase. **[RES]**

**Strategic framing parameters (what must be true):**

1. **The trio's value is discovery access, not a discount.** It delivers three distinct tier experiences — a guided journey from safe to bold, and a way to find a favourite without committing to multiple full-bag purchases. This justifies the ~SGD 50–54 price without competing on cheapness.

2. **The guarantee must be inseparable from the trio.** The freshness guarantee is the answer to the unspoken "but will it be fresh?" question. Wherever the trio appears, the guarantee must be within the same decision frame — the headline offer and the headline risk-reversal are one package. This is especially critical for organic touchpoints (editorial, referral) where the brand cannot control the surrounding context.

3. **All three behavioural segments must find their reason to buy the trio in the messaging:**
   - Adventurers/explorers: the novelty payoff — three coffees they haven't tried.
   - Upgraders: the tasting-flight experience across the full flavour system.
   - Replenishers: a low-risk way to identify their go-to bag.

4. **The trio is Coast's best editorial pitch.** "A Singapore roaster offering a 3-tier Asian-origin discovery box" is a distinctive, concrete story for listicle writers at Honeycombers, TheSmartLocal, SETHLUI, and Time Out. **[RES]** The messaging strategy must produce a story that is *ready for editors to quote* — clear, distinctive, trend-grounded.

### Objection-handling messages (the four key objections)

*Trust is the binding constraint — with no testimonials on file, messaging compensates with risk-reversal, transparency, and a deliberate social-proof harvest plan. **[RES, ADAPT]***

| Objection | Messaging response (strategy) | Proof it leans on | Campaign-specific note **[ADAPT]** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"Will the beans be fresh on arrival?"** | Lead with the **guarantee as a promise the brand keeps, not a hope**: tested, degassed, vacuum-sealed, fresh ≥6 weeks — *guaranteed*. Make risk reversal explicit ("if it's not, we make it right"). | The ≥6-week guarantee + visible vacuum-seal/degassing process [DNA #2] | Strongest owned proof. For organic acquisition — where the buyer hasn't been warmed by an ad — this must be prominent on the landing page, in every editorial mention, and in community conversations. |

The 3-tier system lets anyone start safe, so there is no taste gamble. Pair every bag with a clear, accessible flavour profile. | 3-tier framework + Asian-coffee trend tailwind [DNA #1; RES] | This reframe is inherently pitchable — editors love a "rising trend" angle. Lean into it for earned media. |
| **"Why pay SGD 18–30 vs. the supermarket?"** | Reframe the comparison set to **specialty, not supermarket**: the buyer is paying for curation, testing, the guarantee, and beans matched to their taste. The trio frames cost as *three discovery experiences*, not a per-bag price. | Curation + guarantee + matched-to-taste [DNA #1, 2, 5] | Never compete on cheapness — Hook owns the low end. **[RES]** For replenishers (price-moderate), the trio must feel like fair discovery value, not a premium gamble. |

## 4. Brand personality & voice — UNRESOLVED GAP (customer validation required)

*DNA field: **Brand voice & guardrails: `<TO CONFIRM>`**. The Customer DNA provides no voice guardrails — no tone specification, no words/styles to use, no do's and don'ts. This section is a **gap, not settled strategy**.*

### Why this matters now
Brand voice is not cosmetic. For a cold launch with no testimonials, voice is a trust signal — it either reinforces the "knowledgeable guide" persona that compensates for missing social proof, or it undermines it. Downstream agents (Creative Director, Performance Marketing) cannot lock in copy or creative tone until voice is settled. **[ADAPT]**

### Required before Creative proceeds
The customer must validate or replace the provisional recommendation below. Until then:
- Creative may use the provisional voice as a **working assumption** for early drafts, but must not treat it as final.
- The orchestrator should flag to the customer that **voice is the highest-priority gap to close** — it gates all asset production.

### Provisional recommendation (for customer validation)

**Recommended personality: the knowledgeable guide** — an expert friend who has tasted everything, has strong opinions, and wants *you* to discover what you'll love. Confident enough to stand behind a freshness guarantee; humble enough to let you start safe; specific enough that expertise itself becomes a trust signal.

**Why (unchanged + one addition):** the audience is sophisticated and discovery-minded — upgraders and flavour-adventurers value transparency, provenance, and brew knowledge. **[RES]** They respond to credible expertise, not hype. For an unknown brand with no testimonials, *demonstrated expertise is itself a trust signal* that partly compensates for missing social proof. **[ADAPT]** A guide persona also makes the 3-tier framework feel like helpful curation rather than gatekeeping — critical for the replenisher who doesn't want to feel like they need a coffee certificate to participate.

**Provisional voice traits:**
1. **Knowledgeable, not gatekeeping** — explain origins and flavour like a friend who happens to be an expert; never make the reader feel they should already know.
2. **Warm and plain-spoken** — accessible English; specialty terms are introduced, not assumed.
3. **Confident about the guarantee** — direct and unhedged on freshness; this is where the brand is allowed to be boldest. The guarantee is a commitment, not a caveat.
4. **Curious and discovery-led** — genuinely excited about lesser-known Asian beans; invites exploration without pressure.
5. **Honest and specific** — concrete details (origin, process, flavour notes) over superlatives; never overstate the ≥6-week promise (DNA hard constraint). **[DNA]**
6. **Personally accountable (new trait for this campaign) [ADAPT]** — the small-brand advantage is that there are real people behind the beans. The voice should occasionally speak in the first person ("we tasted this lot and knew it had to be the Tier 3") to signal that real expertise and care sit behind the guarantee, not a faceless operation. This is a trust-substitution tactic for the no-testimonials period.

**Provisional do's and don'ts:**

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Speak plainly; explain flavour and origin in everyday terms | Use coffee jargon as a barrier or status flex |
| Be specific (country, process, tasting notes) | Lean on vague superlatives ("the best coffee ever") |
| State the freshness guarantee directly and confidently | Overstate it beyond ≥6 weeks, or hedge it into meaninglessness [DNA constraint] |
| Invite the nervous buyer to "start safe" | Shame or pressure ("real coffee people drink it black") |
| Frame Asian origins as exciting and ahead-of-trend | Frame them as cheap, exotic, or a novelty gimmick |
| Justify price through value (curation, testing, matching) | Apologise for price or compete on being cheap |
| Use "we" to signal real people behind the brand [ADAPT] | Use "we" to sound corporate or inflated |

**Example phrasings *(illustrative of tone only — NOT approved copy; Creative owns wording)*:**
- *"New to these origins? Start with Tier 1 — easy, reliable, and a great first cup. Ready for something stranger? Tier 3 is waiting."*
- *"We taste every lot before it ships, vacuum-seal it right after degassing, and guarantee it's fresh for at least six weeks. If it's not, that's on us."*
- *"Yunnan, Northern Thailand, the Indonesian archipelago — the coffees most roasters skip. Here's why we think they're worth your morning."*
- *"We're a small team that tastes a lot of coffee — so you only get the ones worth your morning."* **[ADAPT — trust-building via transparency about scale]**

## 6. Strategic parameters (non-negotiable for downstream stages)

*What must be true for this brand strategy to deliver the 70-customer goal. These are constraints and dependencies the orchestrator and downstream agents must honour — not execution instructions.*

### 6.1 The whitespace is the union, not any single element
Coast's defensibility rests on the combination of **(a) multi-country Asian-origin focus, (b) the 3-tier curation framework, and (c) the ≥6-week freshness guarantee.** Any execution that reduces the brand to a single element (e.g., "the Asian coffee people" without the guarantee, or "the freshness people" without the curation) forfeits the whitespace. The three elements must be structurally present together in every major touchpoint — the landing page, the trio offer, the primary campaign message.

### 6.2 The 3-tier framework is the central organising principle
The safe → complex → exciting structure is not a SKU list or fine print. It is how Coast makes its curation promise legible and self-demonstrating. All three behavioural segments — replenisher, upgrader, adventurer — must find their entry point within it. If the framework disappears into the background, the curation differentiator collapses.

### 6.3 The trio must exist, and the guarantee must hold operationally
The entire value proposition depends on these being real. The trio sampler must be available at launch. The ≥6-week freshness guarantee must be operationally deliverable. If either fails, the brand strategy collapses.

### 6.4 All three segments must be addressed — replenisher conversion is not optional
The campaign's mathematics require converting replenishers alongside adventurers and upgraders. Messaging that only speaks to novelty-seekers will struggle to reach 70 customers. The cautious first-timer and the routine-seeker must see themselves in the brand's invitation.

### 6.5 Social proof must exist by week 4
Risk-reversal and transparency are trust substitutes — but they have a shelf life. By week 4 of the 8-week run, real customer reviews, photos, and UGC must be captured from early buyers and deployed publicly. Without them, the organic acquisition flywheel stalls. This is a campaign-execution dependency; the strategy's success depends on it.

### 6.6 The positioning must travel through editorial and community channels
Coast's "Asian-origin discovery" story is inherently pitchable to editors and shareable by early buyers. The brand strategy assumes editorial inclusion in at least 1–2 listicles or features during the 8-week window. This is a mathematical requirement for reaching 25–35 organic customers at the SGD 1,000 paid budget — not a branding "nice to have."

### 6.7 Pricing is premium-justified, never price-led
Coast competes on curation, guarantee, and discovery — never on cheapness. SGD 18–30 per bag and ~SGD 50–54 for the trio must be justified through value (curation, testing, matching), not discounted. Hook owns the low end; cede it willingly.

### 6.8 Asian origins are "ahead of the curve," never second-tier
Unfamiliar origins are reframed as the trend-setting advantage they are — supported by the documented Asian-coffee quality surge (Thailand, Yunnan, APAC growth). Asian beans must never be positioned as cheap, exotic, or inferior substitutes for familiar origins.

### 6.9 DNA constraints are absolute
At most 3 flavour variations. SGD 18–30 per 200–250 g bag. The ≥6-week freshness promise must not be overstated. English-primary (pending customer confirmation of Mandarin/Malay reach).

### 6.10 Voice is gated on customer validation
Section 4 is a provisional recommendation, not settled strategy. The Customer DNA's voice field is `<TO CONFIRM>`. Creative must not lock in final tone or copy until the customer validates the voice recommendation. The provisional "knowledgeable guide" persona may be used as a working assumption for early drafts only.
