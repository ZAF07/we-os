## Executive summary

- **Positioning, in one line:** Coast Coffee is the **Asian-origin discovery roaster for Singapore home baristas** — the only one that organises beans into a **3-tier "safe → unknown" framework** and **guarantees freshness for ≥6 weeks** after degassing, so adventurous brewing by mail stops being a gamble.
- **The core idea is the whitespace, not a single feature.** Research found three things no SG competitor was found to unite: **(a) multi-country Asian-origin focus, (b) the 3-tier curation system, and (c) an explicit ≥6-week freshness guarantee.** [RESEARCH] Coast must own all three together; any one alone is copyable.
- **The 3-tier framework is the spine of the whole strategy** — it is simultaneously the positioning idea, the objection-handler (nervous buyers start at Tier 1), the value-prop pillar, and the structure of the trio offer. [RESEARCH handoff]
- **The hardest problem is trust, not interest.** A cold audience's biggest barrier is "can I trust a new, small brand?" and there are **no testimonials on file** yet. [DNA gap; RESEARCH] Strategy compensates with **risk-reversal** (the guarantee as a promise the brand keeps) and **process transparency** (the testing/degassing process as proof you can see) — substitutes for social proof until first-buyer reviews are harvested during the run.
- **Premium-justified, never price-led.** Hook owns the low end (from SGD 14). [RESEARCH] Coast's messaging must justify SGD 18–30 through curation + guarantee + matched-to-taste, and frame the trio as *discovery value* (three experiences), never as a discount.
- **Recommended voice (to validate):** the **knowledgeable guide** — expert but never gatekeeping; warm, plain-spoken, confident about the guarantee, curious about origins. DNA marks voice `<TO CONFIRM>`, so this is a [REC].

## 1. Positioning statement
*Framework: classic positioning statement.*

> **For** Singapore home baristas who want to brew distinctive coffee at home and keep discovering new beans, **but** are wary of gambling on unfamiliar origins or stale mail-order beans,
> **Coast Coffee** is the **Asian-origin specialty coffee service**
> **that** lets them explore beans from across Asia — matched to exactly how adventurous they feel — with freshness they can count on,
> **because** every bag is sorted into a clear 3-tier framework (safe → complex → exciting), tested before it ships, and **guaranteed fresh for at least 6 weeks** after degassing and vacuum-sealing.

**Category we choose to compete in:** *Asian-origin discovery roaster / curated specialty-bean service* — deliberately **not** "another SG specialty roaster" (a crowded field led by PPP and Common Man on global origins) and **not** a single-country heritage play (Tanamera/JJ Royal own Indonesia-as-heritage). [RESEARCH]

**Why this positioning (tie to the goal):**
- It plants Coast in the **one space research found defensible** — the Asian-origin + 3-tier + guarantee combination no competitor unites. [RESEARCH] For a launch trying to win 70 first-time buyers from a cold audience, a *distinct* claim is what earns the click and the first purchase; a "me-too specialty roaster" claim would lose to brands with more authority and budget.
- The "**but** they're wary…" clause is load-bearing: it names the two first-purchase frictions research validated (freshness-on-arrival, unfamiliar origins) [RESEARCH] and makes the positioning *answer the objection inside the claim itself*, which lowers the perceived risk of a first order — directly serving CAC.
- It reframes Coast's biggest apparent weakness (unfamiliar origins) into the **reason to buy** (discovery), riding the documented Asian-coffee trend tailwind (Thailand/Yunnan rising, APAC fastest-growing). [RESEARCH] "Unfamiliar" becomes "ahead of the curve."

**What we deliberately do NOT claim:** cheapest (Hook owns it), biggest/most-established (PPP owns "#1"), café heritage (Common Man owns it). Competing on those loses. [RESEARCH]

## 2. Value proposition
*Framework: primary value prop + supporting pillars, each tied to a DNA differentiator and a customer job-to-be-done (JTBD).*

### Primary value proposition
**"Adventurous Asian coffee, picked for your taste — and guaranteed to arrive fresh."**
*(stated as strategy, not a finished tagline — Creative owns the wording)*

This single sentence carries all three whitespace elements (Asian + matched-to-taste curation + freshness guarantee) and speaks to the overarching customer job: ***"Let me discover interesting coffee at home without gambling on taste or freshness."*** [DNA pain points; RESEARCH JTBD]

### Supporting pillars

| Pillar | DNA differentiator | Customer JTBD it serves | Why it matters for acquiring 70 new customers |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Pick by how adventurous you feel (the 3 tiers)** | 3-tier flavour framework: safe → complex/floral → exciting/unknown [DNA #1] | *"Match me to coffee I'll actually like — let me start safe and explore at my own pace."* | Converts the cold/nervous majority: the legible "start at Tier 1" on-ramp removes taste-risk, the #1 reason a first-timer hesitates. Also makes the trio self-explanatory. [RESEARCH] |
| **2. Fresh when it lands — guaranteed ≥6 weeks** | Tested, vacuum-sealed after degassing, **≥6-week freshness guarantee** [DNA #2] | *"Don't make me gamble on stale mail-order beans."* | The single most differentiated, hardest-to-copy claim; competitors only promise *roast recency*, not a *guarantee*. [RESEARCH] It is the campaign's primary risk-reversal lever for a brand with no reviews yet. |
| **3. Coffee from across Asia you can't easily get** | Multi-country Asian-origin focus (China, Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia) [DNA #3] | *"Show me coffees I haven't tried and tell me what makes them special."* | The novelty hook that earns attention from flavour-adventurers and rides the Asian-coffee trend. Distinct from Indonesia-only heritage players. [RESEARCH] |
| **4. Brought to your door, matched to you** | Home delivery + beans matched to individual profiles [DNA #4, #5] | *"Keep good coffee arriving so my ritual never breaks — without me travelling to buy."* | Serves the "replenisher" cohort and the convenience-led DTC norm; underwrites repeat/AOV after the first trio purchase. [DNA; RESEARCH] |

**Why this structure:** Pillars 1–3 *are* the three whitespace elements — they make the value prop **defensible**, not generic. Pillar 4 is the table-stakes convenience layer that supports retention/AOV beyond the first sale. Each pillar maps to a real DNA differentiator and a stated/inferred customer job, satisfying the "tie every claim to DNA + a why" rule. **Ranking for a cold launch [REC]:** lead with **Pillar 1 (curation)** to earn interest, prove with **Pillar 2 (guarantee)** to dissolve risk, season with **Pillar 3 (Asian novelty)** to differentiate — because for an unknown brand, *de-risking* the first purchase matters more than novelty alone.

## 3. Messaging hierarchy
*Framework: message pyramid — one primary message → supporting messages → proof, with objection-handlers and offer mapping.*

### Primary message (the one thing to remember)
**"Discover Asian coffee matched to your taste — guaranteed fresh, or it's on us."**
*Why:* it fuses the differentiating idea (Asian discovery, curated to you) with the risk-reversal that compensates for the trust gap, in service of the first-purchase decision. [RESEARCH; DNA] (Wording is illustrative — Creative finalises.)

### Supporting messages
1. **"Start safe or go bold — you choose."** Communicates the 3-tier framework as an invitation, not a taxonomy. Serves the curation pillar and the start-at-Tier-1 on-ramp. [DNA #1]
2. **"Beans from China, Thailand, Indonesia and beyond — the origins most roasters skip."** Communicates the Asian-origin novelty and the trend-tailwind credibility. [DNA #3; RESEARCH]
3. **"We test every bean, then guarantee it's fresh for at least 6 weeks."** Communicates the guarantee + testing process as a concrete, checkable promise. [DNA #2]
4. **"Try all three tiers in one box."** Communicates the trio as the low-risk way to experience the whole system. [Goal offer]

### How messaging maps to the 3 flavour tiers
The tiers are a **messaging device**, not just a SKU list — each speaks to a different mindset and behavioural sub-segment. [RESEARCH segments]

| Tier | Message angle | Speaks to | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — safe & basic** | *"A reliable, crowd-pleasing cup to start with."* | Replenishers / first-timers nervous about taste | Gives the cautious buyer a no-regret entry point — the on-ramp that converts. [RESEARCH] |
| **Tier 2 — complex & floral** | *"Something more nuanced when you're ready to explore."* | Upgraders | Rewards the engaged enthusiast who values nuance and brew control. [RESEARCH] |
| **Tier 3 — exciting & unknown** | *"The bean you can't get anywhere else — for the adventurous."* | Flavour-adventurers | The novelty/status payoff; the clearest expression of the Asian-discovery promise. [RESEARCH] |

### How messaging maps to the trio sampler offer
- **Frame the trio as "the whole system in one box," not a discount.** Its job is to let a cold buyer experience all three tiers in a single low-risk purchase — the category-validated low-commitment entry. [RESEARCH] Messaging emphasises *discovery value* (three distinct experiences, a guided journey from safe to bold), which justifies the ~SGD 50–54 price and protects the premium position. [RESEARCH: not a price undercut]
- **Pair the trio with the guarantee at the point of decision** — "try all three, guaranteed fresh" — so the headline offer and the headline risk-reversal arrive together, maximising first-purchase conversion toward the 70-customer goal.

### Objection-handling messages (the four key objections)
*Trust is flagged as the weakest link; with no testimonials on file, messaging must compensate with risk-reversal and transparency. [DNA gap; RESEARCH]*

| Objection | Messaging response (strategy) | Proof it leans on | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"Will the beans be fresh on arrival?"** | Lead with the **guarantee as a promise, not a hope**: tested, degassed, vacuum-sealed, fresh ≥6 weeks — *guaranteed*. Make the guarantee a stated commitment with risk reversal (e.g. make-it-right if not). | The ≥6-week guarantee + visible vacuum-seal/degassing process [DNA #2] | Strongest owned proof — use it hardest. |
| **"I don't know these Asian origins — are they as good?"** | Reframe unfamiliar → **ahead of the curve**: Asian coffee is the rising trend; and the **3-tier system lets you start safe**, so you're never gambling on taste. Pair each bag with a clear flavour profile. | 3-tier framework + Asian-coffee trend tailwind [DNA #1; RESEARCH] | Turns the objection into the buying reason. |
| **"Why pay SGD 18–30 vs. the supermarket?"** | Reframe the comparison set to **specialty, not supermarket**: you're paying for curation, testing, the guarantee, and beans matched to you — and the **trio frames cost as three experiences**, not a per-bag price. | Curation + guarantee + matched-to-taste [DNA #1,2,5] | Never compete on cheapness — Hook owns it. [RESEARCH] |
| **"Can I trust a new, small brand?"** *(the hardest — trust gap, no testimonials yet)* | Substitute social proof with **(a) risk reversal** (the guarantee = "we take the risk, not you") and **(b) radical transparency** (show the testing/degassing/sealing process so the buyer can verify quality themselves). Speak with specific, confident expertise (see Voice) — a small brand earns trust by knowing its beans deeply, not by claiming scale. | Testing process + guarantee [DNA proof points] | **Proof is thin here.** Strategy must *plan to harvest first-buyer reviews/UGC during the 8 weeks* so social proof compounds — flag to orchestrator/Creative. [RESEARCH] |

## 4. Brand personality & voice *(RECOMMENDATION to validate — DNA marks voice `<TO CONFIRM>`)*
*Framework: brand-personality + voice-trait model. Grounded in the audience research; all of section 4 is [REC].*

### Recommended personality
**The knowledgeable guide** — an expert friend who has tasted everything, has strong opinions, and wants *you* to discover what you'll love. Confident enough to stand behind a freshness guarantee; humble enough to let you start safe.

**Why:** the audience is sophisticated and discovery-minded (upgraders and flavour-adventurers who value transparency, provenance, and brew knowledge). [RESEARCH] They respond to credible expertise, not hype — and for an unknown brand, *demonstrated expertise is itself a trust signal* that partly compensates for the missing testimonials. A guide persona also makes the 3-tier framework feel like helpful curation rather than gatekeeping.

### Voice traits
1. **Knowledgeable, not gatekeeping** — explain origins and flavour like a friend who happens to be an expert; never make the reader feel they should already know.
2. **Warm and plain-spoken** — accessible English; specialty terms are introduced, not assumed.
3. **Confident about the guarantee** — direct and unhedged on freshness; this is where the brand is allowed to be boldest.
4. **Curious and discovery-led** — genuinely excited about lesser-known Asian beans; invites exploration.
5. **Honest and specific** — concrete details (origin, process, flavour notes) over superlatives; never overstate the ≥6-week promise (a DNA hard constraint). [DNA]

### Do's and don'ts

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Speak plainly; explain flavour and origin in everyday terms | Use coffee jargon as a barrier or status flex |
| Be specific (country, process, tasting notes) | Lean on vague superlatives ("the best coffee ever") |
| State the freshness guarantee directly and confidently | Overstate it beyond ≥6 weeks, or hedge it into meaninglessness [DNA constraint] |
| Invite the nervous buyer to "start safe" | Shame or pressure ("real coffee people drink it black") |
| Frame Asian origins as exciting and ahead-of-trend | Frame them as cheap, exotic, or a novelty gimmick |
| Justify price through value (curation, testing, matching) | Apologise for price or compete on being cheap |

### Example phrasings *(illustrative of tone only — NOT approved copy; Creative owns wording)*
- *"New to these origins? Start with Tier 1 — easy, reliable, and a great first cup. Ready for something stranger? Tier 3 is waiting."*
- *"We taste every lot before it ships, vacuum-seal it right after degassing, and guarantee it's fresh for at least six weeks. If it's not, that's on us."*
- *"Yunnan, Northern Thailand, the Indonesian archipelago — the coffees most roasters skip. Here's why we think they're worth your morning."*

## 5. Reasons to believe / proof points
*Marshalling concrete proof, and flagging where it's thin.*

### Strong, owned proof (lean on these)
1. **The ≥6-week post-degassing freshness guarantee.** [DNA #2] The most differentiated claim in the set — competitors offer only roast-recency, not a guarantee. [RESEARCH] Functions as both proof *and* risk reversal.
2. **The bean-testing process.** [DNA proof points] Tested before shipping — a transparency asset the buyer can be shown, substituting for reviews.
3. **Vacuum-sealing after degassing.** [DNA] A visible, physical proof of the freshness promise (you can see the seal).
4. **The 3-tier curation framework itself.** [DNA #1] Evidence that beans are genuinely organised around the buyer's taste, not just stocked — a structural proof of "matched to you."
5. **Multi-country Asian sourcing across China, Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia.** [DNA #3] Proof of the discovery promise; corroborated by the documented Asian-coffee quality trend as external credibility. [RESEARCH]

### Thin or missing proof (flag honestly — do not invent)
- **No customer testimonials, reviews, or results on file.** [DNA `<TO CONFIRM>`; RESEARCH gap] This is the **weakest point** for the hardest objection (trusting a new brand). Strategy compensates via risk-reversal + transparency, but the brand should **actively harvest first-buyer reviews/UGC and the founder's own credibility/credentials during the 8-week run** so social proof exists for later flights. *(Action for orchestrator/Creative — not something this stage can fabricate.)*
- **No baseline metrics** (orders, repeat rate, conversion). [DNA `<TO CONFIRM>`] Limits proof of traction; another reason to capture data during the run.
- **Founder/sourcing story not in DNA.** A credible origin/sourcing narrative would strengthen the "knowledgeable guide" persona and trust — **recommend capturing it.** [REC; not invented here]

## Handoff to Creative Direction
*What the Creative Director Agent must honour (without me doing the creative):*

1. **Lead with curation, prove with the guarantee, differentiate with Asian origin.** The hero idea is the *combination* (Asian + 3-tier + ≥6-week guarantee) — not any single feature. Do not reduce Coast to "fresh coffee" or "Asian coffee" alone; the defensibility is the union.
2. **The 3-tier framework is the central organising idea** — it should structure the creative, the trio presentation, and the on-ramp messaging (safe → bold), not appear as fine print.
3. **Pair the trio offer with the freshness guarantee at the point of decision** — headline offer + headline risk-reversal together, in service of first-purchase conversion.
4. **Trust is the weakest link and there are no testimonials yet.** Creative must do the heavy lifting on trust via **risk-reversal language + visible process/transparency** (testing, degassing, vacuum-seal). Build in a mechanism to **capture first-buyer reviews/UGC during the run.**
5. **Premium, never cheap.** Do not signal discount or compete on price; justify SGD 18–30 / ~SGD 50–54 trio through curation, testing, the guarantee, and matched-to-taste. Hook owns the low end — cede it.
6. **Reframe "unfamiliar Asian origins" as "ahead of the curve,"** using the trend tailwind — never as cheap/exotic novelty.
7. **Voice = the knowledgeable guide** (knowledgeable-not-gatekeeping, warm, plain-spoken, confident on the guarantee, honest/specific) — **pending customer validation** (DNA voice is `<TO CONFIRM>`). Honour the do's/don'ts in Section 4; never overstate the ≥6-week promise (DNA hard constraint).
8. **Stay inside DNA constraints:** at most 3 flavour variations, SGD 18–30/bag band, ≥6-week freshness promise not overstated, English-primary.
